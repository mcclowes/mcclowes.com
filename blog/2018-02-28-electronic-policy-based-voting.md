On the 3rd of May the UK heads back to the polling stations to vote in local elections. Voters will select the councillors that will represent them over, regardless of your optimism, what certainly looks to be one of the most challenging and transformative periods in recent memory.

<!--truncate-->

Polls are indicating dramatic changes in voter intention.

Today, as I do before any election, I visited [ISideWith.com](https://isidewith.com) and took their UK election quiz. This quiz attempts to evaluate which party you should vote for based on how your views align with their policies. 59,324,639 people around the world have used this service to see where their political views lie. I've always found its verdict is pretty spot on.

## How does the quiz work?

Quiz takers answer tens of multiple choice questions, each pertaining to a different policy or issue, grouped into key policy areas. Participants can then rank how much they care about that issue.

An example question might be "Do you believe that UK railways should be nationalised?", and answers range from Yes/No to more nuanced responses.

By comparing your answers' similarity to each party's policy, weighted by how important each issue is to you, the quiz builds a picture of your political view and your best bet on polling day.

## What if that quiz voted for you?

What if that quiz not only showed you which party would likely deliver policy matching your views, but also immediately voted in any upcoming election on your behalf? Would a democracy where support was based purely on policy be a better, truer democracy?

Every election, many people vote not based on the policies of each party but on vague preconceptions on what a given party stands for, likely influenced by media spin and family and friends' voting traditions. Even when people do really consider the policies on offer, it's difficult to go with your gut if that party seems to stand little chance of getting in: tactical voting is rife.

This discrepancy between a voter's views and their vote is something that bothers me, especially as I've seen friends surprised when quizzes like the aforementioned point out how much the party they think they support disagrees with them on so many key issues.

## So what are the positives?

The most obvious advantage is, as with any electronic voting system, ease of voting. Getting to your local polling station can be very inconvenient, particularly in rural areas, and many don't know where to go. Even with an almost 70% turnout in the 2017 UK general election, there is definitely room for improvement, and low voter turnout consistently undermines the democratic process around the world.

Not only would it be easier to vote, but it would be easier to decide how to vote. Unless you closely follow the cacophony of political discourse, getting informed can be difficult — especially when you're constantly being told that everything is 'fake news'. Having a tool accurately take that decision for you could be very helpful.

Any elected party would have a very clear picture of their mandate. Armed with a breakdown of the policies and issues that lead to their electoral victory, it would be easy to see what that party was elected to achieve.

Voting based on policy also opens up the possibilities for more sophisticated voting systems. For example, each citizen could be allocated 100 votes to be distributed across running parties in proportion to the extent to which the voter agrees with their policies.

## The problems with policy

This concept would only work if promised policies were reliably delivered, and parties do not deliver on campaign manifestos. Nor should they have to: policies can quickly become obsolete, as situations and the facts change. Being completely beholden to an unchangeable array of policies could be a dangerous state of affairs.

Nonetheless, this system would require trust in parties, and analysing the voter's trust would require metricisation of trust — easier said than done.

Multiple choice questions lack the nuance required to understand real support, particularly as the extent to which one agrees with a policy likely depends on key details of that policy. For example, at surface level the concept of a simple, unified benefits system, such as Universal Credit, sounds pretty unobjectionable, and it's only once you look at the specifics of the policy that its flaws become obvious.

Finally and critically, voters still need to understand policies and their implications. For instance, saying you support a policy without understanding the implications of that policy doesn't help anyone.

## Tactical voting is a natural part of our system

Tactical voting primarily arises out of the fear of a wasted vote, and comes hand-in-hand with the commonly used first-past-the-post system.

In a proportional voting system, political influence is essentially distributed proportionally based on support, and so every vote a party receives confers equal amounts of influence.

To simplify, first-past-the-post is a 'winner takes all' system, where the party with the most votes gets a (potentially) disproportionate amount of political influence. Every other party gets no influence, as illustrated by UKIP who received 12.6% of the vote in 2017, but only 0.15% of seats in parliament.

Over time, larger parties usually steal voters away from those smaller parties, until two centrist parties remain. Lib Dem, UKIP and Green support has collapsed since the last election — why vote for the Greens when Labour actually stand a chance?

So if our voting system is actually working as expected, maybe this fix isn't even needed.

## Hopefully we won't see systems like this in the near future

Let's be clear, any serious voting should not be conducted through digital means. Tom Scott's Computerphile video, "Why Electronic Voting is a BAD Idea", illustrates this better than I could.

However, given the seemingly inevitable proliferation of technology into every last corner of our lives, electronic systems will become increasingly integral to the democratic process; 17 nations have already seen some form of political voting via electronic means.

## Wouldn't it be great though?

If you could somehow overcome all of the above issues — make sure that parties would actually enact the policies they promised to, ensure voters really understood issues enough to form educated opinions, and stop hackers or corporate interests influencing the election…

Ok, not the best idea.
